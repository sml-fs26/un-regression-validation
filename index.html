CAS BMAI FS26 · Week 1 · coding exercise · un games

The bait, the crash, the patch, the reveal, the trust.

254 countries. 493 features. One target: GDP per capita. Some features carry the economic mechanism the textbook promises — rule of law, life expectancy, internet penetration. Others — Scrabble score of the country’s name, vowel-to-consonant ratio, UNESCO heritage sites — correlate with GDP just as strongly, for reasons that have nothing to do with wealth. Five short chapters walk through the pitfalls of high-dimensional regression by letting you play with the data that teaches them.

causal — a plausible economic mechanism spurious — correlation without cause incidental — real statistic, not a root cause

Five chapters

01 · THE BAIT
What predicts GDP?
Browse the dataset. Scatter any feature against GDP. Rank all 493 by how strongly they correlate. Discover that the Scrabble score of a country’s name can correlate with GDP as strongly as the regulatory quality of its government, and that high correlation is not what you thought it was.
02 · THE CRASH
The hockey stick of doom
Fit OLS with more and more features. Train R² climbs smoothly toward 1. Test R² collapses the moment you cross p=n. Scrub the feature count across the cliff and watch the actual-vs-predicted scatter disintegrate.
03 · THE PATCH
Regularization rescues prediction
Sweep α in Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net. Watch the test score climb out of its crater. Then notice which features Lasso decided to keep …
04 · THE REVEAL
The model kept the wrong features
Lasso’s top-20 coefficients include country-name letter counts and vowel-to-consonant ratios. Random Forest’s top-20 do not. The “best” linear model fell for the same spurious correlations a human would have dismissed in Chapter 01.
05 · THE TRUST
One split lied; what does cross-validation say?
Every number so far came from one random train/test split. Resample. Look at the spread. The point estimate you took as “the answer” was one draw in a distribution wider than you guessed.
