CHAPTER 03 · THE PATCH

A small knob that changes everything.

OLS with 493 features and 203 training rows is an under-determined catastrophe. Ridge, Lasso and Elastic Net add one extra parameter — the regularisation strength α — that penalises large coefficients. Sweep it across a log scale and watch test R² climb from negative numbers into positive territory. Lasso does more: it sets most features’ coefficients to exactly zero.

The three loss functions

OLS minimises the squared error between prediction and truth. With p > n this minimum is reached for many coefficient vectors, and the one OLS lands on is wildly unstable. Each of the three models below adds a penalty term that prefers smaller or sparser coefficient vectors — breaking the tie and stabilising the fit.

Ridge
\[ \hat\beta_{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_\beta \left( \| y - X\beta \|_2^2 + \alpha \|\beta\|_2^2 \right) \]

Penalises the sum of squared coefficients. Shrinks every coefficient toward zero, none to exactly zero. Best when many features are weakly useful.

Lasso
\[ \hat\beta_{\text{lasso}} = \arg\min_\beta \left( \| y - X\beta \|_2^2 + \alpha \|\beta\|_1 \right) \]

Penalises the sum of absolute coefficients. The geometry of the \(\ell_1\) penalty drives many coefficients to exactly zero — making Lasso a feature selector, not just a shrinker.

Elastic Net
\[ \hat\beta_{\text{enet}} = \arg\min_\beta \left( \| y - X\beta \|_2^2 + \alpha \rho \|\beta\|_1 + \alpha (1-\rho) \|\beta\|_2^2 \right) \]

A convex combination of both penalties (we use \(\rho = 0.5\)). Inherits Lasso's sparsity and Ridge's stability when features are correlated.
