## Round 1 · Divergence · Devil's Advocate

### AMPLIFY
> Targeting the brief's "absurd-feature spotlight" (Chapter 4)
> This is the only moment in the whole outline with a pulse. Don't waste it as a "spotlight" — a callout box with an arrow. The 10× version: the model's coefficients are read aloud as a **confession**. One feature at a time, named, weighted, ranked — "GDP is 0.31 × livestock-per-capita, 0.27 × something about goats, 0.19 × a typo in a UN spreadsheet." Make the reader feel the model *incriminate itself*. If Chapter 4 doesn't leave a scar, the whole site is a lecture.

### KILL
> Targeting the brief's "Pearson r bar chart of all 355 features" (Chapter 1)
> 355 bars. Color-coded. We have all seen this image — it is on the cover of every intro-to-ML Medium post written since 2017. Either the 355 features become a **landscape** the reader walks through (a terrain, a shelf, a library, a cemetery), or Chapter 1 has no opening. A bar chart of 355 things is not a visualization, it is a receipt.

### ESCALATE
> Every chapter in this outline is Cartesian. Bar chart, hockey stick, alpha sweep, top-20 ranking, boxplot. Five chapters, zero non-Cartesian ideas. The UN GDP dataset has **countries** in it — objects with shape, location, adjacency, history, absurdity — and the committee is about to render them all as tick marks on an x-axis. I want at least one chapter whose primary visual grammar is **not** a grid of x and y. Maps, constellations, radial layouts, typographic compositions, small multiples of flags, I don't care — break the axis.
