## Round 4 · Cross-pollination · Interaction Designer

### CH1 — The Bait

- ★ **The CSV That Fights You** *(responds to Devil's Advocate ESCALATE + mashes Aesthete's Card Catalog + my Correlation Library + Info Designer's ECDF)*
  - **What:** Opener is the **actual World Bank CSV** — a horizontally scrolling spreadsheet, 356 columns wide, monospace, indigo-on-cream. No brass, no drawer. Column headers are real (`NY.GDP.MKTP.CD`, `FB.CBK.BRCH.P5`, `numerology_capital_letters`). The reader scrolls horizontally and feels the absurd width. A single control: click any column header to sort rows by that column's correlation with GDP. Click another. The first *355 correlations don't separate liars from truth-tellers* — that's the gesture. A thin ECDF strip materializes above the header row as you sort, showing where this column's |r| sits relative to the null band. The "chart" is the header row itself. The "axis" is the CSV.
  - **Why it works:** Dataset-native material answers Devil's ESCALATE without apology. The spreadsheet's horizontal ridiculousness (356 columns is absurd and you feel it with your fingers) *is* the curse of dimensionality, introduced as tactile unease before any math. No metaphor required. The data is the material.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Monospace must stay legible at column density; horizontal scroll must not steal page scroll; reduced-motion shows three pre-sorted states side by side.

- ★ **The Commit Tab** *(mashes Pedagogue's "Bet Your PhD" + Aesthete's Commitment Drawer + my PhD Wager Pipeline — rebuilt in CSV grammar)*
  - **What:** Inside the CSV, two columns are highlighted — labeled `A` and `B`, raw numbers visible. "One of these column values predicts GDP. One is a joke. Click the header you trust." Click commits. The unchosen column header animates to reveal its true name (`rule_of_law_index` / `scrabble_letters_in_capital`). Both correlations type themselves into a new row at the top: `r = 0.79 | r = 0.78`. The reader's wrong column gets a tiny pin-icon in its header that persists through every subsequent chapter's CSV views — they're going to see that pin again in CH4.
  - **Why it works:** Commitment device lives inside the dataset's native interface, not a separate "quiz" overlay. The pin in the header is a dataset-native suspect-board entry — the marginalia is inside the data, not beside it.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Two columns must truly match in shape at display size; pin must survive chapter transitions via persistent state.

### CH2 — The Crash

- ★ **The Column-Adding Gesture** *(amplifies Devil's ESCALATE + mashes Aesthete's Coefficient Storm + Pedagogue's Budget of Surprise + my Feature Dial)*
  - **What:** The same CSV from CH1 is visible. A single control: a small `[+]` button on the rightmost column header that adds one more feature to the OLS fit. The reader taps it. Taps again. Each tap appends a new column to the CSV (you physically see the sheet grow wider) and writes one more coefficient to a sticky row labeled `β̂` at the top. Around column 200 the β̂ row's numbers start rendering in **scientific notation** — the sheet can't hold them. Around column 254 (= n) the β̂ cells begin to **overflow their columns visibly**, digits spilling into neighbors, layout breaking. Test R² below ticks from 0.5 → 0.1 → −3.4. The spreadsheet is literally buckling under its own width. No storm. No pins. No hockey stick. Just a sheet that outgrew its data.
  - **Why it works:** Devil's ESCALATE demanded 21st-century materiality; the answer is that **a spreadsheet breaking is a spreadsheet breaking** — anyone who has ever opened a too-wide CSV feels this in their bones. The coefficient explosion is cell-overflow. The singular matrix is layout collapse. The metaphor is the interface.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Precompute β̂ at every integer p; overflow must be deliberate-looking, not a CSS bug; mobile: the sheet shrinks to a tall strip and overflow happens vertically.

- ★ **Norway's Row Screams** *(mashes Pedagogue's Norway through-line + Info Designer's per-country PI scream + Aesthete's Bleeding Ledger — in CSV grammar)*
  - **What:** As the user adds columns, a single row is pinned to the top of the sheet: Norway. Two cells in Norway's row are `GDP_actual` and `GDP_predicted`. A third cell — `PI_95%` — starts showing `[52k, 54k]` at low p. By p = 200 it reads `[12k, 380k]`. By p = n it reads `[-∞, +∞]` and the cell background flashes red-outline. Norway's prediction never moved much; its uncertainty ate the world. One mono sentence appears beneath the sheet, dataset-native: `# Norway's prediction is still near the point estimate. The confidence interval disagrees.`
  - **Why it works:** Character-driven (Norway stays the protagonist) but rendered as **the exact cell you'd inspect if you were debugging this CSV yourself**. No ledger bleed. No brass. Just a cell with `[-∞, +∞]` written in it — the most honest thing a spreadsheet can say.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** PI must be honestly computed; `[-∞, +∞]` is the scream, don't soften it to `[very wide]`.

### CH3 — The Patch

- ★ **The α Cell, With A 1-SE Annotation** *(replaces the brass scale — responds to Devil's ESCALATE + mashes Aesthete's Brass Scale + Info Designer's CV-error band + my Sweet-Spot Magnet)*
  - **What:** Instead of a tipping brass beam, the α control is **a single editable cell** at the top of the CSV labeled `alpha =`. The reader types a value, or drags the cell's value (spreadsheet-native scrub gesture — click-and-drag on the number to scrub). Beneath the cell, in 9pt mono: `CV_R² = 0.48 ± 0.06 [λ_1SE: 0.02 – 0.31]`. The `[λ_1SE: ...]` is the 1-SE bracket — a *dataset-native* version of the brass detent. Columns whose Lasso coefficient is zero at the current α render as grayed-out headers. The reader scrubs and watches columns gray out, CV_R² update live, and the bracket shift — no "sweet spot magnet" needed because the bracket *is* the tolerance region, visible in monospace.
  - **Why it works:** Answers Devil's ESCALATE: the "sweet spot is a region not a point" lesson is taught by a bracket notation you could type in Python. No brass. The control is a cell. The math is a cell. This is regularization as spreadsheet editing — the grammar the dataset was born in.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Drag-to-scrub on a numeric cell is a known pattern (Observable, Figma) but needs touch adaptation; grayed-out columns must stay visible enough to count.

- ★ **The Null Column Appears** *(mashes Info Designer's shuffled-Y ghost curve + Pedagogue's False Dawn + my Volume Knob ghost station — in CSV grammar)*
  - **What:** As the reader scrubs α, a single extra column appears at the far left of the sheet, striped 45° gray: `GDP_shuffled`. It is the same data with GDP permuted. Every computed quantity (CV_R², number of nonzero β, the 1-SE bracket) has a twin value beside it in dashed gray: `CV_R²_shuffled = 0.11 ± 0.05`. At the reader's chosen α, Lasso keeps 21 real columns and **18 shuffled columns**. The shuffled column count is always visible in the header row, ticking up alongside the real one. The chapter feels like a triumph (real R² climbs to 0.48) but the shuffled twin is quietly climbing too.
  - **Why it works:** Permutation null is a **column in the dataset**, not a ghost overlay on a chart. This is the "null shadow" as spreadsheet literacy — any analyst reading the sheet sees the shuffled number next to the real one and has to reconcile them. The false dawn is planted as a second column, not a scenic cue.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Median of 50 permutations (not one); dashed styling must be unambiguous; the shuffled column must never be hidden — that's the whole point.

### CH4 — The Reveal

- ★ **The Confession As A Growing Row** *(amplifies Devil's Advocate AMPLIFY — structural confession across chapters — mashes Aesthete's Confession + Info Designer's Confession Equation + my Cross-Examined Confession, all in CSV grammar)*
  - **What:** Answers Devil's AMPLIFY structurally. The confession has been typing itself in a sticky **β̂ row** at the top of the CSV since CH1 — just `r=0.78` at first, then partial coefficients in CH2 (with scientific-notation tails), then L1-zeroed cells in CH3 (grayed-out), now in CH4 the row **expands**: each non-zero coefficient cell grows vertically into a stacked mini-cell containing (1) the coefficient value with type-size scaled to |β̂|, (2) a horizontal 200-dot bootstrap selection strip beneath it, (3) a `[object]` button to zero it. Clicking `[object]` strikes the cell, recomputes the whole β̂ row in 200ms, and Lasso substitutes a new column's coefficient to replace it — you watch the model pick another scapegoat in real time. Every objection adds the struck column's header to the reader's pin-strip from CH1. The confession is happening **inside the sheet**.
  - **Why it works:** Devil's Advocate was right — two agents converged on the confession because it is the scar. Making it a *row in the CSV that has been growing since Chapter 1* turns it from a single scene into the site's spine. The reader's objections happen in the same cell where the evidence lives; the struck coefficients are cross-outs in a spreadsheet the reader has been reading for four chapters.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** State persistence across all chapters for the β̂ row; precomputed single-feature-knockout refits for up to ~10 objections; reduced-motion shows the row fully expanded with no animation.

- ★ **The Two-Model Join** *(mashes Aesthete's Dueling Ledgers + Info Designer's selection-stability heatmap + Pedagogue's Lineup Reversed — in CSV grammar)*
  - **What:** Below the main sheet, a second sheet slides up labeled `rf_importances.csv`. The reader performs a **database JOIN gesture** — drag any column header from the Lasso sheet onto a column header in the RF sheet. The drag line draws, the JOIN executes, and a third sheet renders: `feature, lasso_β, rf_importance, agrees`. Only 6 rows have `agrees = True`. The reader has literally performed a SQL INNER JOIN with their cursor and seen the result set. CH1's pinned wrong column appears in this join too — on the Lasso side, absent on the RF side, labeled `your_CH1_pick: null` in the joined sheet. The reader's commitment from chapter one gets its verdict in SQL.
  - **Why it works:** Dataset-native grammar for comparing two model outputs is a join, not vellum threads. The reader performs the comparison themselves with the primitive operation of relational data. The CH1 pin redeems via `null` — the most honest verdict a join can give.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Drag-to-join UX needs clear drop targets; the third sheet must render in < 400ms; the `null` verdict must only appear if the reader's CH1 pick was actually absent from RF top-20.

### CH5 — The Trust

- ★ **The Dissolution, As A Cell That Fills With Values** *(answers Devil's KILL on the boxplot + mashes my Dissolve The Headline + Aesthete's Ghost Splits + Pedagogue's Parallel Universes — in CSV grammar)*
  - **What:** The scene opens with a single mono line at center: `test_R² = 0.52`. A cursor blinks. Then the cell **duplicates** itself — `test_R² = 0.52` → (0.52, 0.47) → (0.52, 0.47, 0.61, 0.29, 0.38, ...). The single value becomes a comma-separated list, the list becomes too long to fit on one line, the list wraps, wraps again, and after 200 splits the cell is a **paragraph of R² values** filling the viewport. Beneath, in the margin of the cell: `mean = 0.34 ± 0.18`. The reader's original 0.52 is bolded inside the paragraph, in one arbitrary position. The boxplot never draws. The headline has become its raw list — **the most honest dissolution a CSV can offer.**
  - **Why it works:** Devil's KILL demanded dissolution, not a boxplot. Dataset-native dissolution is *the cell becoming a column*. A single number revealing itself as a list of 200 is the spreadsheet-grammar inverse of a headline. The reader sees the variance as text. No chart needed.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** The wrapping-paragraph aesthetic must stay austere — same indigo-on-cream as the CSV; reduced-motion shows the final filled cell.

- ★ **The Fold Column You Edit** *(mashes my Fold Painter + Aesthete's Rose Window + Info Designer's per-country error heatmap — in CSV grammar, no rose window)*
  - **What:** A sheet with 254 rows (countries) and one column titled `fold`. All values initially say `random`. The reader can click any cell and assign 1–10. Leave most as `random`; manually fold Norway into `1`, Denmark into `1`, Sweden into `1`. Press run. A second column appears, `test_R²_for_this_fold`, with per-fold results — Scandinavia's fold returns `R² = -0.8`. A third column appears: `norway_predicted_GDP` — for the painted fold, off by 3×. No rose window. No gothic architecture. Just a `fold` column that the reader authored, and its consequences in the next column over. The medallion is a mono sentence beneath: `how you assign the fold column is how you judge the model.`
  - **Why it works:** Devil's ESCALATE satisfied — CV is a column, not a cathedral. Every data scientist has written `df['fold'] = ...` in a notebook. The gesture is the primitive. The prejudice is in the cell values; the consequence is in the next column. Direct manipulation inside the dataset's own schema.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Clicking a cell and typing a value must work on touch; a `randomize fold` button must be present and one-tap.

### X-CUT — Cross-cutting

- ★ **The β̂ Row Is The Whole Site** *(amplifies Devil's AMPLIFY on structural confession + absorbs Aesthete's Marginalia + my Suspect Board + Pedagogue's Scorecard — promotes the marginalia as Devil's KILL demanded instead of cemetery)*
  - **What:** One sticky row at the top of every CSV view across all five chapters. It is the **β̂ row** and it is also the marginalia, the suspect board, and the scorecard. CH1: it holds the reader's committed pick and one correlation. CH2: it overflows with coefficients in scientific notation. CH3: it shows Lasso's zeroed cells as grayed-out and adds a 1-SE bracket. CH4: it expands to the full Confession. CH5: every value in it grows a `± σ` suffix. By the final scroll, the row is a one-line executive summary **the reader watched grow, object to, and witness fail** — screenshotted from the site's own grammar. The final aphorism is a cell in the row: `interpret = False`.
  - **Why it works:** Devil's Advocate KILL + AMPLIFY answered with a single structural element. No cemetery. No separate marginalia track. No separate suspect board. **One row in one sheet, accumulating across chapters, is the site's argument.** The reader's interactions, the model's confessions, the site's facts, and the reader's scorecard live in the same typographic object — a row of a CSV. The site is a spreadsheet that wrote its own critique.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Cell-level state persistence across all chapters; row height must stay roughly constant (≤ 4 lines tall) or mobile breaks; `interpret = False` must feel like a code line, not a tagline.

- ★ **The Two Gestures, Restated As Two Cells** *(responds to Devil's ESCALATE on nine sliders + mashes my Dial Audit + CSV-grammar migration)*
  - **What:** Formal commit: the two surviving live controls across the site are (1) **the `p` cell** in CH2 — a number you click-drag to scrub the column count; (2) **the `α` cell** in CH3 — a number you click-drag to scrub regularization. Both are **editable cells in the β̂ row**. Neither is a slider, knob, dial, or fork. The CH4 objections are clicks inside the same row. The CH5 `fold` column is typing into cells. Every interaction in the entire site is **editing a cell in a spreadsheet** — no brass, no dials, no sliders. The control grammar is the dataset grammar.
  - **Why it works:** Devil's ESCALATE on nine sliders *and* on 19th-century instruments, answered with one commitment: the only interactive primitive is the cell. Sliders imply authored controls; cells imply authored data — which is what the site is about. Two cells, four chapters of consequence, one consistent grammar.
  - **Risk / prerequisite:** Click-drag-to-scrub on numeric cells needs a discoverable affordance (subtle cursor change + microcopy `drag to scrub` on first encounter); touch adaptation is long-press + drag; reduced-motion gives arrow-key nudge.
